• Legend of the Samurai

Weapons


For a long time, the Samurai exist shrouded in mystery. Hollywood action films, fantasy novels and Japanese manga portray these warriors in a very fictional manner.
The media teaches that their swords could cut through stone, and that their secret methods of swordsmithing will never be outdone by modern technology.
It is true that the Japanese Samurai made swords using technology hundreds of years above their time.
It is also true that the Samurai where very formidable warriors, striking fear into their enemies for hundreds of years.

Nihontō (Swords)
Chokutō

Before the invention of folding metal that led to creation of the more notable katana, The chokuto was the samurai's sword of choice. This sword featured a strait blade, in either a single sided or double sided fashion. These swords are known to be similar to the swords found in China around the same time.

Tachi

After the chokuto, the curved tachi followed. Known as the predecessor to the katana, the tachi was curved and typically about 27 inches long. This blade was somewhat fragile and would need frequent maintenance.

Uchigatana A longer version of the tachi, the uchigatana was frequently used to strike from horseback. Also, due to the curvature of its blade - the uchigatana could be drawn and struck in one action rather than the two actions required by the tachi. This fast, deadly strike was practices in many martial arts and samurai circles.

Katana

Typically 27 to 29 inches long, the katana was a curved sword built with special forging techniques. It is theorized some of these swords had their metal folded up to 32,000 times in order to provide the hardest, sharpest blade possible. This forging technique also gives the katana a unique look. The katana could be worn with a smaller sword, a practice known as daisho. The daisho could only be worn by samurai and represented social power and honor. Traditionally, five years of apprenticeship as a blacksmith was required before attempting to create a katana. A blacksmith is only allowed to produce two katanas per month in Japan according to law. Unlike most other swords of its time, the katana is swung with a hacking motion more similar to an axe than a sword. This gives it deadly cutting power and precision, but at a loss of mobility. Most samurai duels with katana would be over after the first strike landed.

Wakizashi

A small sword with a blade between 12 and 24 inches. This sword was worn by samurai in feudal Japan, and differentiated them from common folk. Occasionally, a samurai would request a wakizashi the same length as his katana. This special sized wakizashi is called o-wakizashi. A tanto sized wakizashi is referred to as ko-wakizashi.

Tantō

The tanto, or short blade is one of the traditional Japanese swords worn by the samurai. It features a double edge, and is between 6 and 12 inches long. It was designed as a stabbing weapon, but can be used for slashing as well. Early records suggest the samurai prefered tanto for indoor fighting, where the katana's length was more suited for outdoors engagements.

Yumi (Longbow)
Yumi

Yumi is a term used to describe both the longer daikyu, and the slightly shorter hankyu in Japanese archery. Both of whom where an important weapon of the samurai during the feudal period of Japan. These bows feature a leather grip, and can be up to two meters in height. Many times, the yumi was even taller than the samurai utilizing it. It is also asymmetric, as the grip (nigiri) is positioned three quarters of the way up the bow, in order to ease shooting on horseback. The string (tsuru) was traditionally made of hemp, and the limbs comprised of bamboo.

Yari & Naginata (Spears)
Yari

Derived from Chinese spears, yari are thought to have been from the Nara Period (710-794). These spears where used by samurai, alongside the yumi as weapons of horseback combat. yari had a length of 15 to 20 feet, and a blade ranging from several inches to three feet.

Naginata

Similar to yari, the naginata instead featured a curved blade. It is similar to the Chinese guan dao, or the European glaive. It featured a hand guard (tsuba), similar to that found on the katana. The blade was removable so it could be stored for safe keeping when not in use.

Bo, Jo, & Hanbo (Staffs)
Staff Weapons

The 10 foot bo, 6 foot jo, and 2 foot hanjo where all fighting sticks the Japanese samurai built after encountering the Chinese kon. Still used widely today in martial arts, these staves served a special purpose in self defence. One of the first "non-lethal" weapons, these where usually made out of hard wood such as red oak, white oak or bamboo. Rattan was used for training variations, due to its increased flexibility. Although the samurai would come to wield these weapons on occasion, they are most often attributed to the Okinawa monks who utilized it for self defence after the ban of weapons outside of the samurai class.

Jutte, Kanabō (Clubs)
Jutte

Jutte are characterized by their short demeanour and round metal blade. Similar to sai, these weapons where used mainly by the Japanese police during the Edo Period. These where typically used as blunt bashing weapons similar to a baton, but could also be used for stabbing and slashing.

Kanabō

The Kanabo was a spiked club used by samurai and their retainers. Usually made out of heavy wood, or entirely from iron - the Kanabo was very heavy and dangerous. It use is believed to be smashing armour, and the legs of enemy warhorses.

Kusarigama & Kusari-Fundo (Chain-Weapons)
Kusarigama

The kusarigama, or chain sickle is comprised of a metal chain with a weight at the end. Attacking usually involved swinging the weight overhead, than whipping it forwards to entagle the enemie's spear or sword.

Kusari-Fundo

A hand held weapon consisting of a chain, and a weight connected to each end. Usually 12 to 48 inches, this weapon was used by the samurai as a non-lethal alternative when making arrests. One popular nickname for this weapon was the "ten thousand power chain
